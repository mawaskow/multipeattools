SECTION: Key Statistics
ICON: 📊
Belgian peatlands are estimated to cover less than 1% of the country’s surface area, with estimates ranging between 13,000 - 24,780 hectares of peat soils. These are mostly located in the country’s eastern higher-altitude Ardennes region, notably in the Hautes Fagnes region, where the Zwarte Beek valley contains about 4,000 hectares of peatlands alone.

HIGHLIGHT: Important Note
Over 60% terrestrialising peat in Belgium is dominated by Sphagnum spp., and its mires support other important vegetation including Eriophorum spp., cranberries (Vaccinium oxycoccos), and andromeda (Andromeda polifolia).

LIST: Key Points
- About 90% of active raised bogs are considered to have disappeared since the 15th century.
- Nitrogen pollution & tourism led to the loss of ¼ peatlands in the Hautes Fagnes and Plateau des Tailles since 1957.
- Purple moor grass (Molinia caerulea) dominates peatlands, preventing other native
  wildlife from growing.

SECTION: Conclusion
ICON: 📚
Several projects for the protection and rewetting of Belgium’s peatlands have been taking place, particularly in the Hautes-Fagnes region. the Zwarte Beek valley is undergoing a project to restore 100 hectares of peatland which is estimated to reduce 2,500 tons of CO2 per year over 50 years.
